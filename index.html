The Beauty of Schottky Groups

Katelin P. Beach and Peter E. Francis Möbius Transformations are a convinent group of maps to study in an introductory course on trasnformational geometry. We will explore a specific kind of subgroup of Möbius transformations and how they are used to make fractal-like images.
Introduction
We are motivated by understanding the following picture from the perspective of transformational geometry.
An Apollonian Gasket
Apollonian gaskets are named after the Greek mathematician Apollonius of Perga. To construct one, first draw three mutually tangent circles and circumscribe a fourth circle tangent to all three. Then, ad infinitum, for each region inside the fourth circle but outside any other, inscribe the largest possible circle. Recall that Möbius transformations $T$ are transformations of the extended complex plane (the Reimann Sphere) $\C^+$ of the form $$T(z)=\frac{az+b}{cz+d}$$ for complex constants $a, b, c, d\in\C$ satisfying $ab-cd\neq 0$. These transformations form the Möbius group $\M$. It turns out that the key to understanding pictures like the Apollonian Gasket above is to study specific subgroups of $\M$. Kleinian Groups are discrete subgroups of $\M$, and these groups are studied in generality, but we'll focus on a particular kind of Kleinian group called Shottky Groups, which are easier to define in the context of an introductory course in transformational geometry.
Pairing Clines
It will be usefult to say that two clines are "non-overlapping" if they intersect at most once. It is important to keep in mind that because we are working in the extended complex plane, there is no real distinction between the "interior" and "exterior" of a single cline. We are still going to use the words, but it won't be confusing. If the cline in question is a circle, your intuition won't lead you astray. When we refer to the interior of a line, it will always be in a very specific context: we have a set of non-overlapping clines, and there will be a side the line that does not contain the interior of another, and this region is the line's interior. In short, the interior of a cline cannot intersect the interior of another. Are we still being ambiguous about interiors and exteriors?
Four non-overlapping clines and their interiors shaded gray
Given two clines $C$ and $C'$, suppose a Möbius transformation $T$ maps $C'$ to $C$, and also takes the exterior of $C'$ to the interior of $C$. Then $T$ is said to pair the cline $C'$ to $C$. In the definition above, $T$ must also take the interior of $C'$ to the exterior of $C$. Clearly, if $T$ pairs $C'$ to $C$, then $T^{-1}$ pairs $C$ to $C'$. There is a unique cline $K(C, C')$ over which (by inversion) $C$ and $C'$ are symmetric. Constructing Schottky Groups
Schottky groups were first studied by Friedrich Schottky in 1877. A Schottky group is a Kleinian group generated by transformations $T_1,\dots, T_n$ where there are $2n$ disjoint simple loops, $C_1, C_1',\dots,C_n, C_n'$, bounding a common domain $F$ in the extended complex plane $\C^+$, where $T_i(C_i') = C_i$, and $T_i(F)\cap F=\emptyset$, $i = 1,\dots,n$. For our purposes, we will assume that the loops are clines in $\C^+$. Furthermore, (like the authors of ) we'll allow for the possibility that the clines in the definition above touch, but not overlap. The resulting Kleinian group is called a "kissing" Schottky Group. In this case, $F$ might not be connected! The clines $C_i$ and $C_i'$ might be thought of as defining clines for the Schottky group, however, they do not completely determine the group. Indeed, recall from our discussion of pairing clines that any hyperbolic transformation can be inserted in between the two inversions. Consider a set $\mathcal{C}$ of $n$ pairs of (overall) non-overlapping clines: $$\mathcal{C}=\{(C_1,C_1'), \dots, (C_n,C_n')\},$$ and the associated set of pairing transformations (as defined earlier) $$\T=\{T_1, \dots, T_n\}.$$ Define the "swiss cheese"-looking set $$F=\C^+-\bigcup_{i=1}^n \overline{D_i \cup D_i'}$$ where $D_i$ and $D_i'$ are the interiors of $C_i$ and $C_i'$. Intuitively, $F$ is the extended plane with the clines and their interiors punched out. Let $G$ be the Schottky group generated by $\T$ ($G=\langle \T\rangle$), and observe that for any $T_i,T_i'\in G$, $T_{i}\circ T\in G$ maps $F$ into (but not onto) the interior of $T(D_i)$ and similarly $T_{i}'\circ T\in G$ maps $F$ into (but not onto) the interior of $T(D_i')$. The orbit $\O_F$ of $F$ "tiles" the plane, except for the point-set boundary of $\O_F$, which is the limit set of $G$. That is, $$\partial \O_F = \C^+ - \O_F.$$ The orbit $\partial \O_F$ is called the ordinary or regular set of the group. Also, due to the contstruction of $\T$, for any $T\in G$, $T(\partial \O_F) = \partial \O_F$. If $\partial \O_F$ is a circle then it is called Fuchsian; if $\partial \O_F$ is a loop, then it is called a quasicircle. A Kleinian Group whose limit set is a quasicircle is called a quasifuchsian group. The limit sets of quasifuchsian groups separate the ordinary set into two disjoint parts. A Schottky Group $\langle a, b\rangle$ generated with two pairs of circles is quasifuchsian if and only if
  • the tangency points must be correctly matched by generators;
  • the transformation $aba^{-1}b^{-1}$ must be parabolic;
  • the trace of $aba^{-1}b^{-1}$ must be $-2$.

Generating Images of Regular and Limit Sets
Let's start with the orbit of $F$, which "tiles" the regular set: we'd like to see the outlines of the puzzle pieces. Luckily, this is pretty simple since images of $F$ are bounded by clines. One way to get a picture of $\O_F$ is to rely on the fact that groups generated by $n$ transformations can be thought of free groups on $n$ symbols: longer words in the associated free group correspond to smaller images of $F$ in the orbit. Therefore, we can do a depth-first search through the branches of the free group and prune nodes when we are deep enough down the tree and/or the circles to be plotted become too small to see. This is exactly what you can do on the generator page. Enter a list of circles and specify pruning depths and/or circle sizes. It is a bit trickier to explicitly plot the limit set. If we plot the tiling $\O_F$ to a significant depth, we see that the limit set is where the smallest circles accumulate, but how can we just get the limit set by itself? If we know a little bit about the limit set, we can cheat a bit. For example, if $\partial \O_F$ is comprised of circles, we can plot the orbit of a specially chosen few of them. That is how we get the image below of the Apollonian Gasket. Limit Sets of Kleinian Groups
The study of Kleinian groups in generality started with Henri Poincaré and Felix Klein in 1883. Given a Kleinian group $G$, we write $\Lambda(G)$ for its limit set, the set of limit points of the set $\{T(x)\mid T\in G\}$, where $x$ is any point in hyperbolic three-space. The complement of the limit set is called the discontinuity or regular set of $G$ and is denoted $\Omega(G)$. It is pointed out in that $\Lambda(G)$ is either
  1. finite with cardinailty at most 2,
  2. uncountably infinite and perfect nowhere dense, or
  3. equal to $\C^+$.
We will build to some examples of the second case, which is clearly the most interesting and (perhaps, unsurprisingly) the most beautiful.
