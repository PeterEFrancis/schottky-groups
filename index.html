Pairing Clines
It is important to keep in mind that because we are working in the extended complex plane (the Reimann Sphere), there is no real distinction between the "interior" and "exterior" of a cline. We are still going to use the words, but only to refer to opposite sides of a cline; it won't be confusing. Given two non-overlapping clines $C_1$ and $C_2$, a MÃ¶bius transformation that maps the exterior and interior of $C_1$ to the interior and exterior of $C_2$ is said to pair the circles. Specifically, if $T_2$ maps $C_1$ to $C_2$, then we say that $T_2$ pairs $C_1$ to $C_2$. Of course, this means that $T_2(C_1)=C_2$. The inverse map $T_1=T^{-1}$ acts similarly on $C_2$; this action is "symmetric." There is a unique cline $K(C_1, C_2)$ over which (by inversion) $C_1$ and $C_2$ are symmetric. Write this Any transformation $T$ that pairs $C_1$ to $C_2$ can be written as the composition of the following maps:
  • Invert over $C_1$
  • Transform $C_1$ to the unit circle
  • Perform any transformation from the hyperbolic group (disk automorphism)
  • Transform the unit circle back to $C_1$
  • Invert over the cline $K(C_1,C_2)$
For now we will consider the case when the transformation chosen from the hyperbolic group is the identity. Algebraically, suppose $C_1$ and $C_2$ have centers $p_1$ and $p_2$, and radii $r_1$ and $r_2$. Then any function $T_2:\C^+\to\C^+$ that pairs $C_1$ to $C_2$ is of the form \begin{align*} T_2(z) & = \left(I_{K(C_1, C_2)} \circ I_{C_1}\right)(z) \\ & = \frac{r_1^2}{\overline{\left(\frac{r_3^2}{\overline{z - p_3}} + p_3\right) - p_1}} + p_1 \\ & = \frac{r_1^2}{\frac{\overline{r_3^2}}{z - p_3} + \overline{p_3-p_1}} + p_1 \\ & = \frac{r_1^2(z-p_3)}{\overline{r_3^2} + (\overline{p_3-p_1})(z-p_3)} + p_1 \\ & = \dots \\ & = \left(\frac{r_1^2}{\overline{p_3-p_1}}+p_1\right)\frac{z - \left(\frac{r_1^2p_3+\overline{r_3^2} + p_3\left(\overline{p_3-p_1}\right)}{r_1^2+p_1\left(\overline{p_3-p_1}\right)}\right)}{z-\left(\frac{\overline{r_3^2} - p_3\left(\overline{p_3-p_1}\right)}{\overline{p_3-p_1}} \right)} \\ \end{align*} where we define $C_{p_3}(r_3)=K(C_1, C_2)$. It is easy to see that $T_2$ is a loxodromic transformation, and that $C_1$ and $C_2$ are type II clines for the two fixed points of $T_2$. Show that the mess in the numerator is the image under inversion over $C_1$ of the mess in the denominator.
Schottky Groups
A Kleinian group is a discrete subgroup of the group of transformations on the Reimann Sphere. Consider a set $\mathcal{C}$ of $n$ pairs of non-intersecting clines: $$\mathcal{C}=\{(C_{1,1},C_{2,1}),\dots, (C_{1,n},C_{2,n})\},$$ and the associated set of pairing transformations $$\T=\{T_{2,1},\dots, T_{2,n}\}.$$ Define the "swiss cheese"-looking set $$F=\C^+-\bigcup_{i=1, j=1}^{2,n} D_{i,j}$$ where $D_{i,j}$ is closed disk associated with cline $C_{i,j}$. Intuitively, $F$ is the extended plane with the clines and their interiors are punched out. Let $G$ be the (Schottky) group generated by $\T$, and observe that $T_{i,j}\circ T\in G$ maps $F$ into (but not onto) the interior of $T(D_{i,j})$. The orbit $\O_F$ of $F$ tiles the plane, except for the point-set boundary of $\O_F$, which is called the limit set of $G$. That is, $$H = \partial \O_F = \C^+ - \O_F.$$ Also, due to the contstruction of $\T$, for any $T\in G$, $T(H) = H$.
Conjecture. $\partial \O_F = \O_{\Phi(\T)} = \Phi(G)$, where $\Phi(X)$ defines the fixed point set $\{p\in\C^+\mid \exists T\in X, T(p)=p\}$.
Conjecture. $\Gamma:\mathcal{K}\to\mathcal{K}$ given by $\Gamma(X)=\O_X$ is a contraction mapping with fixed point $H$.
