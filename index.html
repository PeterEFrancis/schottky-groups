Schottky Groups
Given two non-overlapping circles $C_1$ and $C_2$, there is a loxodromic MÃ¶bius transformation $T$ that maps the exterior and interior of $C_1$ to the interior and exterior of $C_2$, respectively; $T$ maps $C_1$ to $C_2$. Clearly, the inverse map $T^{-1}$ acts similarly on $C_2$; this action is "symmetric," and we say that $T$ pairs the two circles.
If $C_1$ and $C_2$ have centers $p_1$ and $p_2$, and radii $r_1$ and $r_2$, then the transformations $T$ given by $$T(z) = \frac{-r_1r_2}{\overline{z-p_1}} + p_2$$ pairs the circles. It is easy to see that $C_1$ and $C_2$ are type II clines for the fixed points of $T$.
Now, consider a set $\mathcal{C}$ of $n$ pairs of circles: $$\mathcal{C}=\{(C_{1,1},C_{1,2}),\dots, (C_{n,1},C_{n,2})\},$$ and the associated set of pairing transformations $$\T=\{T_1,\dots, T_n\}.$$ Define the "swiss cheese"-looking set $$F=\C^+-\bigcup_{i=1, j=1}^{n,2} D_{i,j}$$ where $D_{i,j}$ is closed disk associated with circle $C_{i,j}$. Intuitively, $F$ is the extended plane with the circles and their interiors are punched out.
