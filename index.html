An Apollonian Gasket
Recall that Möbius transformations $T$ are transformations of the extended complex plane (the Reimann Sphere) $\C^+$ of the form $$T(z)=\frac{az+b}{cz+d}$$ for complex constants $a, b, c, d\in\C$ satisfying $ab-cd\neq 0$. These transformations form the Möbius group $\M$. It turns out that the key to understanding pictures like the Apollonian Gasket above is to study specific subgroups of $\M$. There is a more general concept that includes the types of groups we are going to explore, but we will focus on a particular kind of group that is easier to define in the context of an introductory course in transformational geometry.
Pairing Clines
We will say that two clines are "non-overlapping" if they intersect at most once. It is important to keep in mind that because we are working in the extended complex plane, there is no real distinction between the "interior" and "exterior" of a single cline. We are still going to use the words, but it won't be confusing. If the cline in question is a circle, your intuition won't lead you astray. When we refer to the interior of a line, it will always be in a very specific context: we have a set of non-overlapping clines, and there will be a side the line that does not contain the interior of another cline, and this region is the line's interior. Are we still being ambiguous about interiors and exteriors? Given two clines $C$ and $C'$, suppose a Möbius transformation $T$ maps $C'$ to $C$, and also takes the exterior of $C'$ to the interior of $C$. Then $T$ is said to pair the cline $C'$ to $C$. In the definition above, $T$ must also take the interior of $C'$ to the exterior of $C$. Clearly, if $T$ pairs $C'$ to $C$, then $T^{-1}$ pairs $C$ to $C'$. There is a unique cline $K(C, C')$ over which (by inversion) $C$ and $C'$ are symmetric. Schottky Groups
Schottky groups were first studied by Friedrich Schottky in 1877. A Schottky group is a Kleinian group generated by transformations $T_1,\dots, T_n$ where there are $2n$ disjoint simple loops, $C_1, C_1',\dots,C_n, C_n'$, bounding a common domain $F$ in the extended complex plane $\C^+$, where $T_i(C_i') = C_i$, and $T_i(F)\cap F=\emptyset$, $i = 1,\dots,n$. The loops $C_i$ and $C_i'$ are the defining loops for the Schottky group, however, they do not completely determine the group. Indeed, recall from our discussion of pairing clines that any hyperbolic transformation can be inserted in between the two inversions. For our purposes, we will assume that the loops are clines in $\C^+$. Consider a set $\mathcal{C}$ of $n$ pairs of (overall) non-intersecting clines: $$\mathcal{C}=\{(C_1,C_1'), \dots, (C_n,C_n')\},$$ and the associated set of pairing transformations (as defined earlier) $$\T=\{T_1, \dots, T_n\}.$$ Define the "swiss cheese"-looking set $$F=\C^+-\bigcup_{i=1}^n \overline{D_i \cup D_i'}$$ where $D_i^{(\prime)}$ is the largest open disk bounded by the cline $C_i^{(\prime)}$ so that no two disks intersect. Intuitively, $F$ is the extended plane with the clines and their "interiors" punched out. Let $G$ be the Schottky group generated by $\T$, and observe that $T_{i}^{(\prime)}\circ T\in G$ maps $F$ into (but not onto) the interior of $T(D_i^{(\prime)})$. The orbit $\O_F$ of $F$ tiles the plane, except for the point-set boundary of $\O_F$, which is the limit set of $G$. That is, $$H = \partial \O_F = \C^+ - \O_F.$$ Also, due to the contstruction of $\T$, for any $T\in G$, $T(H) = H$.

Limit Sets of Kleinian Groups
The study of Kleinian groups in generality started with Henri Poincaré and Felix Klein in 1883. A Kleinian group is a discrete subgroup of $\text{PSL}(2,\C)$, the group of orientation-preserving conformal homeomorphisms of the extended complex plane, and as the group of orientation-preserving isometries of hyperbolic 3-space. A Kleinian group is a discrete subgroup of the group of transformations on the Reimann Sphere. Given a Kleinian group $G$, we write $\Lambda(G)$ for its limit set, the set of limit points of the set $\{T(x)\mid T\in G\}$, where $x$ is any point in hyperbolic three-space. The complement of the limit set is called the discontinuity or regular set of $G$ and is denoted $\Omega(G)$. It is pointed out in that $\Lambda(G)$ is either
  1. finite with cardinailty at most 2,
  2. uncountably infinite and perfect nowhere dense, or
  3. equal to $\C^+$.
We will build to some examples of the second case, which is clearly the most interesting and (perhaps, unsurprisingly) the most beautiful.
