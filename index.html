Schottky Groups
Given two non-overlapping circles $C_1$ and $C_2$, there is a loxodromic MÃ¶bius transformation $T_2$ that maps the exterior and interior of $C_1$ to the interior and exterior of $C_2$, respectively; $T_2$ maps $C_1$ to $C_2$. Clearly, the inverse map $T_1=T^{-1}$ acts similarly on $C_2$; this action is "symmetric," and we say that $T_2$ (and $T_1$) pairs the two circles.
If $C_1$ and $C_2$ have centers $p_1$ and $p_2$, and radii $r_1$ and $r_2$, then the transformation $T_2$ given by $$T_2(z) = \frac{-r_1r_2}{\overline{z-p_1}} + p_2$$ pairs the circles. It is easy to see that $C_1$ and $C_2$ are type II clines for the fixed points of $T_2$.
Now, consider a set $\mathcal{C}$ of $n$ pairs of circles: $$\mathcal{C}=\{(C_{1,1},C_{2,1}),\dots, (C_{1,n},C_{2,n})\},$$ and the associated set of pairing transformations $$\T=\{T_{2,1},\dots, T_{2,n}\}.$$ Define the "swiss cheese"-looking set $$F=\C^+-\bigcup_{i=1, j=1}^{2,n} D_{i,j}$$ where $D_{i,j}$ is closed disk associated with circle $C_{i,j}$. Intuitively, $F$ is the extended plane with the circles and their interiors are punched out.
Let $G$ be the (Schottky) group generated by $\T$, and observe that $T_{i,j}\circ T\in G$ maps $F$ into (but not onto) the interior of $T(D_{i,j})$. The orbit $\O_F$ of $F$ tiles the plane, except for the point-set boundary of $\O_F$, which is called the limit set of $G$. That is, $$H = \partial \O_F = \C^+ - \O_F.$$ Also, due to the contstruction of $\T$, for any $T\in G$, $T(H) = H$.
Conjecture. $\partial \O_F = \O_{\Phi(\T)} = \Phi(G)$, where $\Phi(X)$ defines the fixed point set $\{p\in\C^+\mid \exists T\in X, T(p)=p\}$.
Conjecture. $\Gamma:\mathcal{K}\to\mathcal{K}$ given by $\Gamma(X)=\O_X$ is a contraction mapping with fixed point $H$.
